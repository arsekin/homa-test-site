My research revolves around AI for Social Good, with a major focus on evaluating the scale and dynamics of problems of societal importance. This ranges from measuring bias in mainstream media to auditing online platforms for harassment, polarization, and algorithmic biases.

The overarching theme of my work is utilizing computational tools — machine learning, natural language processing, complex network analysis, and statistical inference — to extract actionable and interpretable insights from data, typically with very little or no supervision. General features of my research on the study of digital technologies include carefully considering the whole spectrum from pure user intention to pure platform design, performing cross-platform analysis, and putting observations into context when studying complex systems. My work is inherently interdisciplinary and I collaborate closely with sociologists, economists, psychologists, and political scientists.

Over the years, my research has been featured in the press and published in more than 30 peer-reviewed papers, including computer science conferences and top journals such as PNAS, Science Advances, TKDE, and IMWUT.

Research

My current work studies the information ecosystem, focusing on a subset of problematic content to determine where we may encounter and consume such information. To quantify and measure these concepts, I am working on developing methods for high-quality inference from text at scale.


1) What is defined as problematic: From misleading and biased to extreme and fake

  • In my early works, I focused on misbehavior as one sort of problematic content online, from milder cases of aggression to extreme cases of bullying. In my future research, I will expand by studying the full spectrum of a new line of problematic content across audience sizes, ranging from traditional media with misleading and biased portrayals of information to extreme political content and low quality information sources. There has been ample work on fake news and misinformation on text-based platforms such as Twitter and Facebook, while multimodal information sources such as YouTube and TikTok have been overlooked due to not being known as political information sources and difficulty accessing and processing their data. Thus, the scope of problematic content hosted on these platforms is unclear. Building a dataset of more than 34 million videos from six years of real browsing records, we are currently studying what fraction of the news content on YouTube can be considered low quality, conspiratorial, or biased against specific groups or societal issues. While our recent work shows that this category of content is small in both scale and audience size, the extreme consequences of radicalization demand significant attention.

    Another understudied facet of the information ecosystem is the scale of misleading and biased information in traditional media and its impact on society. In a case study of COVID narratives, Oxford University found a significant amount of misleading information from TV and mainstream outlets, specifically regarding policy actions of public authorities. Another recent work has shown that small differences in wording influence study participants to view certain groups as socially distant and less human. These studies reveal that biased information — not even necessarily extreme — can have real-world impacts ranging from micro-aggression and harassment to more serious violence. In one of my recent works, program-level comparisons of cable news and broadcast networks over the last 10 years revealed differences in the scale of coverage and the narratives around certain controversial topics. In future work, I will examine the evolution of gender, rational, and ethical biases utilizing language models.


2) Where do we get our information from?

  • While determining the scale of problematic content is the necessary first step, understanding how much attention it is given will provide a full picture of its potential to alter beliefs, behavior, and policies. In my recent work, I have studied the precise size and scope of news echo chambers and their dynamics in TV, text-based, and video-based platforms using large, individual-level, observational panel data. In recent years, video-based platforms have become increasingly popular, and hard news — which is typically used in media studies — is not the only source of political information. I plan to evaluate Americans’ news diet diversity more fully, combining browsing data from the mobile, web (including text-based and video-based platforms), radio, and TV along with the transition dynamics among these sources over time. I also intend to look at content-level shifts; for example, if a viewer who regularly watches FOX News on the TV transitions to YouTube, would they continue watching the same content, or shift from FOX to anti-woke YouTube content?


3) How to quantify concepts: Developing computational methods

  • Most of the existing research on misinformation and fake news is at the source level — for example, all the content produced by a web publisher or YouTube channel that is labeled conspiratorial is considered to be the same. In reality, content quality is mostly mixed, and it is not clear how much factuality varies across different sources. In my future work, I will address this concern utilizing my computational expertise in complex network analysis, applied machine learning, and natural language processing. Unsupervised techniques such as topic models have been widely used for the exploration of large-scale text data. Quantitative validation of inferences made from such models is a crucial step when investigating social questions, yet it is overlooked. In order to address this issue, I intend to examine the diversity of unsupervised topic modeling outcomes and how these differences can be utilized to minimize uncertainty when using unsupervised techniques, as well as the degree to which topic level evaluation metrics (topic coherence or intrusion) are associated with errors in topic assignment to data points.
